\section{Industrial Organization, Week 7 Individual Answers}

Suppose we have two kinds of consumers. Consumers of type A have a utility function $U_a=10 q_A - q_A^2 \frac{1}{2}$. Consumers of type B have a $U_B=5 q_A - q_A^2 \frac{1}{2}$



I will do some exposition here because I did not have the time to detail this out in the lecture. So as shown in the lecture if the cost of production is $0$ and consumers are uniformly distributed between $0$ and $1$. If the monopolist cannot discriminate, they will maximize $p(1-p)$ and find a price equal to $p=\frac{1}{2}$. Which will result in profit of $\frac{1}{4}$. The consumer surplus is then, $\frac{1}{8}$, and the deadweight loss is, $\frac{1}{8}$. (recall that since production costs are $0$, the perfect competition outcome is everyone pays $0$)

If the monopolist can discriminate between consumers at $0-0.5$ and those $0.5-1$. The he will maximize, the sum of the upper segment and lower segment, $\frac{1}{2}p_2(1-p_2)+\frac{1}{2}p_1(\frac{1}{2}-p_1)$. Notice that we will take two partial derivatives here and set them both equal to $0$. $p_1 = \frac{1}{4}; p_2 = \frac{1}{4}$. 

1) Compute the deadweight loss and consumer surplus
2) Now suppose the firm has finer information and can partition the consumers in into the intervals $0-\frac{1}{3}$, $\frac{1}{3}-\frac{2}{3}$, and $\frac{2}{3}-1$. What will the price be for every segment and what is the resulting deadweight loss/profit/consumer surplus. (Note that simply deriving may get you the wrong answer, you have to remember that for EACH segment, there is no point in setting the price LOWER than the HIGHEST willingness to pay)

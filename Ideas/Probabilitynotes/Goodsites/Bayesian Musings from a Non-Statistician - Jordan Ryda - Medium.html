101 Recap

  1. ‚ÄúProbability and statistical inference have inverse objectives‚Äù

With probability modelling we set the parameters for a specified model and simulate potential outcomes. Statistical inference on the other hand ingests measurements from the world and backtracks to estimate the parameters of a chosen model.

The bidirectional relationship underpins the way we validate statistical techniques‚Äô potential to capture the essence of reality: we synthesise data from a known distribution, blur the signal with some noise that represents random sampling error and estimate the true parameters, point estimates.

Similarly, Bayesians criticise and revise their models by analysing a posterior predictive distribution (c.f point estimates from before). These ‚Äúposterior predictive checks‚Äù examine the deviation of data simulated by the fitted posterior model from the observed data.

2. ‚ÄúConditional probabilities can be visualised‚Äù

It can be helpful to consider probabilities as having geometric representations ‚Äî as areas within a complete probability space. Conditional probabilities ‚Äúcollapse‚Äù the probability space from the ‚Äúuniverse‚Äù to that which the hypothesis is conditioned on.

3. ‚ÄúAll classical hypothesis testing is asking is whether the evidence makes the null hypothesis look ridiculous‚Äù

Hypotheses are uncertain descriptions of what the world looks like. They can can either be discrete (innocence or guilt, which die did I roll) or continuous (mean level of gene expression). In either case Bayesians think of assigning probability distributions (prior and posterior) to hypotheses. There is nothing fundamentally different between the prior and the posterior ‚Äî they represent your state of knowledge at any point in the process with the data you‚Äôve observed so far.

More specifically we throw data into the likelihood (by initialising the random variable to these fixed observed values) so that it may ‚Äútug‚Äù on the prior distribution of each of the likelihood‚Äôs parameters (both functions of the unknown parameters, see Appendix A).

Primary Frequentist Differences

  1. ‚ÄúStatistics is the science of changing your mind‚Äù

Frequentist statistics is characterised by changing your mind about default actions based on the ‚Äúunsurprising-ness‚Äù of the data (the [in]famous p-value). Bayesians change their minds about prior beliefs instead. Frequentists need not form a belief to have a default action ‚Äî this is purely something one commits to without analysing any data.

2. ‚ÄúThe source of uncertainty‚Äù

Inference from a frequentist‚Äôs perspective says that sampled data is drawn from a large or infinite sample space that we may take expectations over. The randomness of data gives the standard error in regression coefficient estimates ‚Äî uncertainty in what would happen to the estimates if we observed more data sets that look like our own. This sampling error is the variance in a classifier‚Äôs predictions as discussed in more explicit machine learning contexts (linear regression is a part of the ML toolkit after all). A high variance means you‚Äôve overfit on your one sample and have claimed to make sense out of the randomness present.

Bayesians reason instead that a probability distribution represents the unknown-ness of a quantity, not the randomness of the quantity from the data. The uncertainty in our state of knowledge of the system is described by our strength of ‚Äúbelief‚Äù about the true and unknown parameter values for the observed data thus far.

Being pedantic, while the source of uncertainty is different, Bayesians may agree with frequentists that the ‚Äútrue‚Äù parameter takes a fixed value that we would like to know, whether fixed from the start or potentially generated from a physically random mechanism. There are however instances in Physics, say the uncertainty principle, where we nature fundamentally can only ever reveal a distribution.

Since frequentists don‚Äôt regard the unknown parameter as a random variable, this means that P(ùêª0|‚Äúanything‚Äù) makes no sense in classical hypothesis testing; the truth of a particular hypothesis is not a random variable but is true or it‚Äôs not. It is one of the mistakes in suggesting that a p-value is the ‚Äúprobability that an observation is due to chance‚Äù: statements like P(ùêª0|data) only have meaning in a Bayesian context (but more strikingly the the p-value is computed assuming the null hypothesis is true ‚Äî that sample differences are caused by random chance).

Some Ah-ha!s

1. ‚ÄúBayes‚Äô theorem ‚â† Bayesian inference. The first is deductive, the second is statistical and uses probability distributions‚Äù

Bayes‚Äô theorem is not a Bayesian approach per se. The theorem is a formula foundational to probability theory that connects inverse conditional probabilities. Rather what makes one a Bayesian is quantifying uncertainty with probability over hypotheses. Inference, or updating ones‚Äô beliefs, involves reallocating credibility across hypotheses given observations.

It‚Äôs worth prefacing that only in the most trivial cases do data have definitive and deterministic relations to candidate causes i.e. we can make an observation and completely rule out one or more possibilities. In reality inferences are probabilistic (conditional probability tables say in Bayesian networks do not contain 0s or 1s). Rather the data relate to their underlying causes with degrees of certainty (and the measurement process blurs the underlying generating process with noise).

Nonetheless, if we have complete knowledge of the probability space of mutually exclusive hypotheses ‚Äî if we know exacts priors and likelihoods for all hypotheses ‚Äî then Bayes‚Äô formula computes the posterior exactly. Such is the case when we roll a die that is randomly selected from a cup whose contents we already knew. This is deductive logic of probability theory, and it gives a direct way to compare hypotheses, draw conclusions, and make decisions.

In the cookie jar problem, where the identity of the jar (A, B, ‚Ä¶) is the outcome being estimated, it is trivial to get to the correct probability using natural frequencies without ever appealing to the theorem (30/[30+20]). We can get to the same answer using Bayes‚Äô theorem if we consider the person selecting the cookie as part of the system and treat the identify of the jar as a hypothesis quantified by a random variable itself. In this case we must assume that a random choice is made and therefore uniform prior probability is spread across the hypotheses of jars. The Monty Hall problem presents a similar case.

In most experiments, the prior probabilities over hypotheses are not known. In this case, our recourse is the art of statistical inference: we either introduce a prior (Bayesian) or do our best using only the likelihood (frequentist).

If we estimate our probabilities with sample proportions and plug them into Bayes‚Äô formula this is still not Bayesian inference since it produces a point estimate. True Bayesian inference is used when we turn to using probability distributions for the prior and likelihood over hypotheses instead of point estimates.

2. ‚ÄúHow Bayesian reasoning can give us both ‚Äúcorrect‚Äù but also ‚Äúsubjective‚Äù probabilities‚Äù

It follows from above that we can that Bayes‚Äô theorem can allow us to reason using deductive logic of probability, giving us access to the conditional probability of real interest. For example the the proper probabilistic treatment of Sally Clark‚Äôs innocence uses Bayes‚Äô theorem to access the correct conditional value. We can similarly take the canonical disease screening example and recognise our property of interest is P(D|+ve) and not P(+ve|D) we note that tests are not the event.

Most would agree with the above regardless of their statistical persuasion. Now to introduce the subjective part. The Bayesian interpretation of probability as a credibility specific to an individual with a certain state of knowledge doesn‚Äôt sit well with some frequentists ‚Äî should science accept that different individuals with identical data may sensibly reach different conclusions?

However it can be argued that the likelihood is also subjective ‚Äî Bayesian modellers commit a priori to a joint distribution of the data and parameters, p(Œ∏, data), and not simply the prior. Nonetheless the frequency guarantees of confidence intervals and uncertainty quantification of credible intervals converge once there is enough information.

3. ‚ÄúPrecision and Recall are directly related through Bayes theorem‚Äù

This becomes clear if we define precision and recall as conditional probabilities, for disease present D and positive test +ve.

Precision: P(D|+ve)

Recall: P(+ve|D)

Read from right to left, precision asks ‚Äúgiven a +ve test, what‚Äôs the probability the disease is present‚Äù while recall asks ‚Äúgiven a disease present, what‚Äôs the probability the test is +ve‚Äù. These are two very different things and wrong to assume that a screening test with high recall implies a high precision too ‚Äî this is the prosecutor‚Äôs fallacy (explained visually next). One can have 100% recall but low precision due to false positives i.e. P(D|+ve) ‚â™ P(D|+ve). More realistically, the product of a small disease prevalence (large P(D‚Äô)) with a non-insignificant false alarm rate P(+ve|D‚Äô) can reduce the value of precision far below that of the recall.

4. ‚ÄúThe effect of a small prior done visually‚Äù (the prosecutor‚Äôs fallacy)

A well-reasoned concern was published by a heart doctor on the new ECG feature on the Apple. The crux of his piece rested on the rarity of people with atrial fibrillation (about 1%) which would cause false alarms, and many false alarms given the number of sales one can expect from a company that generated more profit in 3 months than Amazon has ever done. The red line below left should be committed to memory by anyone who builds or uses classifiers or diagnostic tests; small priors have a dramatic effect on false positives even with tests that have a specificity = sensitivity = 99%!

One way to understand why this is is to consider a confusion matrix whose areas are proportional to a quadrant‚Äôs probability. If we artificially vary the prior D (normally a fixed estimate from the data) toward 0 we get a precision 1/(1+FP/TP) that tends to 0 as TP falls quickly due to its change in area whilst FP increases.

It‚Äôs worth pointing out that while precision (and NPV) are dependent on the population being tested ‚Äî influenced by the prevalence of disease ‚Äî sensitivity, P(+ve|D), and specificity, P(-ve|D‚Äô), are conditioned on the diseased/healthy state (they are determined horizontally above).

ML tangent: Since recall and specificity are conditioned on the true class label, ROC curves are invariant to the baseline probability (class imbalance) and this is a key distinction from PR curves for which precision is dependent. Where baseline probabilities of the problem are important, and the ‚Äúpositive‚Äù class is more interesting than the negative class, PR curves may be more useful in characterising classifier performance since they are more sensitive to class imbalance.

Another way to see the effect of a small prior is by representing the probabilities as Venn diagrams which allow the fractions to be visualised more readily.

The graphic shows again how a rare disease (small red area) with a test that has 99% sensitivity/recall (large intersection within red) and 99% specificity (large white area) can still yield many false positives and hence low precision (large green area).

5. Empirical Bayes: ‚ÄúLook at your data then set your priors‚Äù

Empirical Bayes is useful in ranking and analysing a large list of proportions such as CTRs and gene expression levels. Knowledge of the entire distribution is brought into each estimate and shrinks the credible intervals whilst pulling the expected values toward it, particularly those observations with smaller amounts of data. It may be viewed as an approximation to a fully Bayesian treatment of a hierarchical model (see below) where the hyperpriors are set to their most likely values, instead of being integrated out.

6. ‚ÄúPrior information can formally inject common-sense into the equation‚Äù

In the contrived coin toss experiment with coin of bias p, the Bayesian‚Äôs input of common sense manifests as a ‚Äúhead start‚Äù in their maximum a posteriori estimate of the coin‚Äôs bias for a Beta distribution of potential biases,

with number of successes s and failures f. For a uniform prior, Beta(Œ±=1,Œ≤=1), we revert to the intuitive maximum likelihood answer of s/(s+f) from the pure frequentist who assumes that the sample data is representative of the population of coin tosses because that‚Äôs all there is. This makes a difference with small sample sizes. ‚Äú4 heads and 0 tails you say? The coin is 100% biased‚Äù.

However it should be said that since Bayesians have a full posterior distribution over parameter values there is a preference for expected values ‚Äî integrating the full posterior to account for full uncertainty and potential multi-modality.

7. ‚ÄúPrior information can regularise a model‚Äù

Lasso regression constrains large coefficient estimates and shrinks some towards zero, effectively performing variable selection and choosing a simpler model. This avoids overfitting and is much better suited in dealing with datasets that contain collinearity.

Another way to phrase this is that we have a prior belief that most coefficients should be close to zero, and this can be expressed through a prior distribution (rather than through a cost function with a penalty term). This is exactly what can be achieved using Bayesian regression with the appropriate priors.

8. ‚ÄúUncertainty in your neural network predictions‚Äù

Bayesian deep learning aims to move from point estimates of weights to full distributions for all latent variables. As a by-product we get to propagate through the uncertainty of random variables to the uncertainty of the prediction. Note that we effectively double the number of parameters we must learn (mean and spread).

Wide and deep neural networks represent more complex Bayesian models that can contain millions of parameters and in these instance Monte-Carlo methods are slow to converge ‚Äî they may take weeks to discover the full posterior. To help solve this a technique called Variational Inference has been gaining momentum through probabilistic programming libraries such as Uber‚Äôs Pyro and Google‚Äôs Edward2.

MCMC

  1. ‚ÄúMonte Carlo techniques are not just for Bayesians‚Äù

What‚Äôs well known is the use of bootstrap resampling to compute the standard error in an arbitrary statistic using sample data without ever having seen the parent or population distribution. But what is less well known is that random sampling can be used to produce a much more flexible null hypothesis significance test (NHST) framework than an ‚Äúoff-the-shelf‚Äù analytical model.

With such a framework you have full choice over the test statistic and are forced to make your model assumptions explicit in setting up the simulations. It can be useful to prototype different test statistics and models that may turn out to yield different results ‚Äî something potentially noteworthy. Packages such as infer do just that.

For example, if you wanted to compute the p-value of the Hamming similarity between two vectors you could:

  1. Randomly shuffle your two vectors and compute the Hamming similarity (the null hypothesis is that the vectors are unrelated)
  2. Repeat step 1 many (N) times and measure the number of times (x) that the simulated statistic is larger than the observed value of the two original vectors

The proportion of times that the simulated vectors were more similar than the original vectors, x/N, is the (one-sided) p-value by definition.

2. ‚ÄúWe still care about the normalising constant P(data) in sampling problems‚Äù

If we only care about point estimates in our posterior (e.g. find the MAP) we can drop P(data), a normalising constant, since we just care about exploring the parameter space to maximise likelihood * prior. This is an optimisation problem: find Œ∏ that maximises the proportionality. P(data) is equally irrelevant if doing hypothesis comparison on the same dataset using the Bayes Factor.

This situation is different for MCMC sampling problems. A denominator is necessary to squeeze out fully-fledged probabilities. This is a sampling distribution problem: find P(Œ∏|data), which requires integration over the entire sample space. This can be computationally difficult!

3. ‚ÄúThe intractability of the normalising constant‚Äù

Notice how marginalising out Œ∏ in the denominator is the same problem as attempting to normalise the numerator with an unknown constant so that it integrates to 1. This is by deliberate setup of a ‚Äúprobability‚Äù ‚Äî from the law of total probability the numerator will be a subset of the denominator that defines the scope of the entire space.

Consider a system of binary variables (say quantum spin states in the Ising model) where we seek a probability mass function of the spin configurations. Calculating the integral in the denominator (where x is the data) can be difficult even here since to produce a normalised probability we must sum over all of the possible spin configurations which increases exponentially with the number of variables. For a small 10 x 10 grid this is 2¬π‚Å∞‚Å∞ different configurations to calculate the average magnetisation.

MCMC is our heuristic knight in shining armour ‚Äî it allows us to sample from a distribution g(x)/k without knowing or caring about k and it does this by stepping through the probability space with a weighted random walk that‚Äôs biased toward the more likely states.

AB Testing

  1. ‚ÄúHypothesis testing = Parameter estimation‚Äù

To answer questions that are posed as hypothesis tests, we can elegantly transpose the question to be one of parameter estimation and inferring the HPD. This style feels much more natural and in this way we get the effect sizes between groups. The PyMC3 library uses deterministic random variables (values fully determined by their parent values) to handle all the transformations necessary under the hood that ultimately output the desired P(B-A > 0).

2. ‚ÄúMagnitude is more important than statistical significance‚Äù

NHST (say a Chi-square test or logistic fit) can at most tell us ‚Äúthese two things are not likely to be the same‚Äù (unless we bake a difference threshold into our hypotheses).

When running a test we‚Äôre often aiming to not just understand whether a variant will perform better, but by how much. For example, if testing whether a drug will improve IQ more than a placebo, if we have achieved a boost of 3 IQ points, even within our posterior credibility interval, can we really say that the drug is effective and worthy of approval?

3. ‚ÄúThe frequentist approach is more risk averse in decision making‚Äù

The primary focus of NHST is avoiding Type I errors; there is a preference to stick to default actions (the null hypothesis is assigned with this assumption). If a new recommendation algorithm is only slightly better than the current model but has a p-value of 0.11, we might be tempted to discard it. However not all false positives are created equally. For example, choosing variant B with CTR 8% and A is 8.3% is a very different mistake to make than if A were 15%. Again it‚Äôs the magnitude that is also of interest.

4. ‚ÄúRemind me, what‚Äôs a p-value again?‚Äù

Interpretability is crucial in communicating ideas and articulating byzantine concepts like p-values takes some care. Would you rather report to your boss that ‚Äúthe probability that A is greater than B is 7%‚Äù, or perhaps ‚Äúassuming the null hypothesis that A is equal to B, the probability that we would see a result at least this extreme in A versus B is 3%‚Äù?

Bayesians are also better equipped to posit stronger statements to make decisions from e.g. P(B-A > Œ¥) where Œ¥ can be any difference we specify (set Œ¥=0 for any improvement).

Hierarchical Modelling

  1. ‚ÄúAssumption: observations are related to one another‚Äù

Hierarchical modelling can be seen as a middle-ground between pooling all measurements together and creating separate regressions for treatment groups. It is distinct from empirical Bayes since we are no longer estimating hyperpriors but are modelling their uncertainty through a hyperprior distribution.

Through such a design we impose a model structure with our assumptions that our treatment groups of observations are related to one another. Depending on the scenario this can be sensible, say justified using the biological concept of common descent. Everything we use to justify our model structure comes from assumptions extrinsic to the data ‚Äî domain knowledge ‚Äî that we still have to qualitatively and quantitatively verify. There is no free lunch; we have to describe our assumptions explicitly and encode them in the model structure or in the probability distributions used e.g. you might ultimately justify a parsimonious model following Occam‚Äôs razor.

‚ÄúAll models are wrong, but some are useful‚Äù ‚Äî George E. P. Box

2. ‚ÄúRare species are not alone‚Äù

When we have a new sample for which we have very few observations we are able to borrow evidence from the rest of the population to make more tightly-bound inferences about the new sample. In some cases the collection of new data is simply not feasible e.g the beak length of a new rare species of bird.

A non-hierarchical design in this scenario will give a nonsensically wide credible interval (characterised by the high posterior density [HPD] interval) for a rare species. This is because we impose identical uninformative priors on each species that ‚Äúlet the data speak for itself‚Äù thus leaving us with large uncertainties. In the non-hierarchical case, the prior parameters are point estimates and not drawn from a parent distribution.

3. ‚ÄúThe generative story‚Äù and ‚Äúinformation pooling‚Äù

The generative story is as follows: the population as a whole has a parental hyperprior distribution that constrains the region from which the prior parameters of individual species independently draw. We could justify this assumption when looking at different species that share a single common ancestor, or when inferring the bias of different coins produced by the same mint.

What makes hierarchical modelling effective is that all the data is used to estimate the parental distributions‚Äô parameters which in turn guides the estimates of the individual species‚Äô parameters. If all the joint priors between an individual parameter and the parental hyperprior show strong association, then we see that the individual posteriors are strongly influenced by each other‚Äôs data (see Appendix C).

If we were to simplify the above chain of dependencies such that only Œº depended on a hyperprior (i.e. take the central chain only) for each species we would get:

P(œÉ,Œº|y) ~P(y|œÉ,Œº)P(œÉ,Œº) = P(y|Œº)P(Œº|œÉ)P(œÉ)

  • By the definition of a hierarchical model we have a factorised chain of dependencies, one for each distribution in the chain
  • The data only depend œÉ in the sense that when œÉ is set then the data are independent of all other parameter values. In other words the likelihood function P(y|Œº) does not involve œÉ, by model definition. Similarly the value of Œº depends on the value of œÉ and is conditionally independent of all other parameters
